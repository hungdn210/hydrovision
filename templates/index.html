Extreme Event Analysis

Fit GEV and Gumbel distributions to annual maxima. Compute return levels for 2–200 year events with bootstrap confidence intervals.

Include Analysis ?
Methodology
Block maxima The annual maximum value is extracted for each calendar year, forming the extreme-value sample. This follows the block maxima approach of classical extreme value theory (EVT).
Distributions GEV (Generalized Extreme Value) is the general family encompassing Gumbel, Fréchet, and Weibull tails via the shape parameter ξ. Gumbel is a special case (ξ = 0) assuming an exponential tail — simpler but less flexible for heavy-tailed hydrology.
Estimation Parameters fitted by Maximum Likelihood Estimation (MLE) using SciPy. MLE is asymptotically efficient and is the standard for EVT in hydrology.
Confidence intervals Bootstrap CI with 150 resamples of the annual maxima block. Bands widen substantially for rare events (>50 yr) and should be interpreted as indicative uncertainty bounds, not precise probabilistic guarantees.
Return levels A T-year return level is the value expected to be exceeded on average once every T years, i.e. annual exceedance probability = 1/T. These are statistical estimates from the fitted distribution — not hydraulic flood routing or inundation modelling.
Limitations Results depend on record length. Short records (<30 yr) yield wide CIs for rare events. Stationarity is assumed — if the climate has shifted, historical return levels may underestimate future risk.

Change Point Detection

Identify statistically significant regime shifts in hydrological time series using PELT or Binary Segmentation algorithms.

3
136

Methodology
What it detects Structural breaks where the mean, variance, or trend of the series shifts abruptly — indicating a regime change such as dam construction, land-use change, or a climate shift.
PELT Pruned Exact Linear Time. Finds the globally optimal segmentation by minimising a penalised cost function. Faster than BinSeg and exact — preferred when record length is large or speed matters.
BinSeg Binary Segmentation. Recursively splits the series at the single strongest break, then repeats on each sub-segment. Approximate but flexible — useful when breakpoints may be closely spaced.
Penalty Both algorithms use an L2 (mean-shift) cost with a BIC-style penalty to avoid over-segmentation. The max breakpoints slider caps the upper bound of detected breaks.
Interpretation Detected break dates are statistically optimal, not causal. Always cross-reference with known events (dams, droughts, gauge relocations) before drawing hydrological conclusions.

STL Decomposition

Separate a hydrological series into Trend, Seasonal, and Residual components using Seasonal-Trend decomposition via LOESS (STL).

Include Analysis ?
Methodology
Pre-processing Raw data is resampled to monthly means before decomposition. This stabilises the seasonal period to exactly 12 steps and reduces sub-monthly noise.
STL algorithm Seasonal-Trend decomposition via LOESS (Cleveland et al. 1990). Iteratively fits a smooth trend and a repeating seasonal component using locally-weighted regression, leaving the residual as the unexplained remainder.
Robust fitting Robust mode down-weights outliers during LOESS fitting, preventing flood spikes or data gaps from distorting the estimated trend and seasonal pattern.
Strength indices Trend strength FT and seasonal strength FS are computed as 1 − Var(residual)/Var(trend+residual) and 1 − Var(residual)/Var(seasonal+residual) respectively, following Wang et al. (2006). Values near 1 indicate a dominant component; near 0 indicates noise dominates.
Interpretation The trend component captures multi-year changes (e.g. long-term drying, river regulation effects). The seasonal component captures the annual flood–drought cycle. Large residuals may indicate extreme events, data errors, or non-periodic forcing.
Wavelet Analysis

Identify time-varying periodicities (annual cycle, ENSO, decadal oscillations) using the Continuous Wavelet Transform with a Morlet wavelet.

Include Analysis ?
Methodology
Transform Continuous Wavelet Transform (CWT) computed via PyWavelets using the complex Morlet wavelet (ψ). Unlike Fourier analysis, the CWT resolves both frequency content and its location in time simultaneously.
Morlet wavelet A Gaussian-windowed complex sinusoid. Well-suited for hydrology because it balances time and frequency resolution, making it sensitive to both sharp events (floods) and slow oscillations (ENSO, decadal cycles).
Scalogram The 2-D heatmap shows CWT power (|W|²) across time (x-axis) and period (y-axis). Bright regions indicate that a given periodicity was energetically dominant during that time window.
Cone of influence Edge effects arise because the wavelet extends beyond the record boundaries at large scales. The cone of influence (COI) marks the region where edge effects become significant; results outside the COI should be interpreted with caution.
Global spectrum The global wavelet power spectrum averages CWT magnitude over the full time dimension, producing a single power-vs-period curve analogous to a smoothed Fourier periodogram. Peaks indicate persistent dominant cycles across the record.
Hydrological signals Expected peaks: ~12 months (annual monsoon cycle), ~24–36 months (ENSO forcing), ~6–8 years (ENSO modulation by PDO), ~10–20 years (decadal climate variability). Intermittent peaks indicate non-stationary periodicities.
